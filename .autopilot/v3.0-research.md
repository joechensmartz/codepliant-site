## 1. Article 50 Deep Dive: Transparency Obligations

Source: [EU AI Act Article 50](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/article/50/) | [AI Act Service Desk — Article 50](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-50)

### Article 50(1) — Human-AI Interaction Disclosure

Providers must ensure that natural persons are informed they are interacting with an AI system, **unless this is obvious from the point of view of a reasonably well-informed person**. Exception: systems authorized by law to detect/prevent/investigate criminal offences (unless publicly available for reporting crimes).

**Codepliant implication:** Our AI Disclosure generator should include a section stating the product uses AI and that users are informed of this. This is the baseline transparency obligation.

### Article 50(2) — Synthetic Content Machine-Readable Marking

Providers of AI systems that generate synthetic audio, image, video, or text must:
- Mark outputs as "artificially generated or manipulated" in **machine-readable format**
- Solutions must be **"effective, interoperable, robust and reliable as far as this is technically feasible"**

Exceptions: assistive editing functions, systems not substantially altering input, law enforcement authorized systems.

**Codepliant implication:** If a user's product generates synthetic content (images, audio, video, text), we need to flag this and recommend C2PA/Content Credentials or watermarking. This is a new section for our AI Disclosure document.

### Article 50(3) — Emotion Recognition / Biometric Categorisation

Deployers of emotion recognition or biometric categorisation systems must **inform exposed individuals about the system's operation** and comply with GDPR. Exception: law enforcement with appropriate safeguards.

**Codepliant implication:** If we detect emotion-recognition or biometric libraries (e.g., face-api.js, AWS Rekognition), we should flag this as a special disclosure requirement.

### Article 50(4) — Deep Fakes

Deployers of AI systems that generate or manipulate content constituting a deep fake must disclose artificial creation. Also applies to AI-generated text published to inform the public on matters of public interest. Limited exceptions for:
- Artistic, creative, satirical, or fictional works
- Content subject to human editorial review with clear editorial responsibility

**Codepliant implication:** If we detect image/video generation libraries (Stable Diffusion, DALL-E, Midjourney integrations), we should add a deep fake disclosure section.

### Article 50(5) — Accessibility & Timing

Information must be provided:
- **"In a clear and distinguishable manner"**
- **"At the latest at the time of the first interaction or exposure"**
- Must meet **accessibility requirements** (Directive (EU) 2019/882)

**Codepliant implication:** Our generated disclosure should include guidance that it must be shown before/during first user interaction, not buried in settings.

### Article 50(6-7) — Relationship to Other Laws

- Does not supersede Chapter III (high-risk) requirements or other transparency laws
- AI Office should encourage voluntary codes of practice for labeling AI-generated content

### Key Enforcement Date

**August 2, 2026** — All Article 50 transparency obligations become fully enforceable. Fines: up to EUR 35 million or 7% of global annual turnover.

## 2. Real AI Disclosures: How Companies Do It

### Notion AI

**Source:** [Notion AI Security & Privacy Practices](https://www.notion.com/help/notion-ai-security-practices) | [Notion AI Safety](https://www.notion.com/help/ai-safety)

**Sections included:**
1. What is Notion AI? (overview of capabilities)
2. How does Notion AI work? (technical description)
3. Data Protection (encryption, security)
4. Controls & Settings (user opt-in/out)
5. Legal Considerations (terms, IP ownership)
6. Compliance Standards (SOC 2, etc.)

**Key disclosure language:**
- "Notion and its AI Subprocessors do not use Customer Data to train any models" (by default)
- Maintains public Subprocessor Page listing all third parties (Anthropic, OpenAI)
- Enterprise: zero-retention APIs; Non-enterprise: 30-day max retention
- Customers can subscribe to subprocessor change notifications

**Risk classification:** Not explicit risk tiers, but differentiates Enterprise vs non-Enterprise data handling.

### Canva

**Source:** [Canva Shield](https://www.canva.com/newsroom/news/safe-ai-canva-shield/) | [Canva AI Product Terms](https://www.canva.com/policies/ai-product-terms/) | [Canva AI Safety](https://www.canva.com/policies/ai-safety/)

**Sections included:**
1. AI features overview (Text to Image, Magic Write, Magic Edit, Translate)
2. Privacy settings and user control
3. Creator compensation program ($200M over 3 years)
4. Training data practices (creators can opt out)
5. Content safety policies

**Key disclosure language:**
- Users "always in control of their AI and privacy settings"
- Creator opt-out available for AI training
- Transparency Reports published for government data requests

**Risk classification:** Not explicit. Focus on user control rather than risk tiers.

**Criticism:** Details about AI data use reportedly "hidden in fine print."

### Grammarly

**Source:** [Grammarly Responsible AI](https://www.grammarly.com/ai/responsible-ai)

**Sections included:**
1. Five guiding principles: Transparency, Fairness, User Agency, Accountability, Privacy & Security
2. Intentional product development (checks, balances, evaluations)
3. Data safeguarding (encryption, no third-party training)
4. User control (accept/reject suggestions, disable features)
5. Practical applications (education-focused guidance)

**Key disclosure language:**
- "We do not sell or monetize user content"
- "People are the ultimate decision-makers"
- AI as "decision-support tool" — emphasizes augmentation over replacement

**Risk classification:** Comprehensive AI risk assessment process evaluating privacy, security, fairness, safety, abuse potential. Responsible AI team categorizes features by risk level. Every shipped feature requires documented mitigation strategies.

**Issues:** Faced class action lawsuit alleging AI Expert Review feature misled customers into believing human editors reviewed their work. Had to pause AI cloning of experts without explicit consent.

### Adobe

**Source:** [Adobe Content Credentials](https://helpx.adobe.com/firefly/web/get-started/learn-the-basics/content-credentials-overview.html) | [Adobe Gen AI User Guidelines](https://www.adobe.com/legal/licenses-terms/adobe-gen-ai-user-guidelines.html) | [Partnership on AI Case Study](https://partnershiponai.org/adobe-framework-case-study/)

**Sections included:**
1. Content Credentials (digital "nutrition label" for AI content)
2. Training data transparency (licensed Adobe Stock + public domain)
3. User guidelines and restrictions
4. IP indemnification for commercial use
5. Content safety restrictions

**Key disclosure language:**
- Content Credentials auto-attached to 100% AI-generated Firefly content
- "Generated outputs sometimes may be inaccurate or misleading"
- Users cannot use outputs to train other ML systems
- "May attach or publish Content Credentials" — not always guaranteed

**Risk classification:** Not explicit tiers, but strong C2PA commitment. Adobe is a founding member of C2PA (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity).

**Industry leadership:** Adobe's approach is the gold standard for synthetic content marking, directly implementing what Article 50(2) will require.

### GitHub Copilot

**Source:** [GitHub Copilot Trust Center](https://copilot.github.trust.page/) | [GitHub Copilot Trust Center FAQ](https://copilot.github.trust.page/faq) | [GitHub Copilot Business Privacy Statement](https://docs.github.com/en/site-policy/privacy-policies/github-copilot-business-privacy-statement)

**Sections included:**
1. Security (SOC 2, ISO 27001)
2. Privacy (data handling, retention)
3. Compliance (certifications)
4. Transparency (Responsible AI principles)
5. FAQ (detailed Q&A format)

**Key disclosure language:**
- Follows Microsoft's Responsible AI Standard (6 principles: accountability, transparency, fairness, reliability & safety, privacy & security, inclusiveness)
- Business/Enterprise: "does not use data to train models"
- Prompts discarded once suggestion returned; suggestions not retained
- User engagement data retained 24 months
- Opt-out available for telemetry

**Risk classification:** Follows Microsoft's Responsible AI Impact Assessment framework.

## 4. Machine-Readable Marking & Technical Standards

### EU Code of Practice on Marking & Labelling AI-Generated Content

The European Commission published the first draft (Dec 2025), with a second draft in March 2026 and final version expected June 2026, ahead of the August 2, 2026 enforcement date.

Source: [EC — Code of Practice on AI-generated content](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/code-practice-ai-generated-content) | [EC — Second Draft](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/library/commission-publishes-second-draft-code-practice-marking-and-labelling-ai-generated-content)

### Three-Tier Marking Framework

The Draft Code mandates a **multi-layered approach** — no single technique suffices for Article 50(2) robustness:

1. **Metadata Embedding (C2PA Standard)**
   - Embeds provenance information into file metadata
   - Includes: provider name, system version, creation timestamp, unique identifier
   - Fragility concern: metadata can be stripped during screenshots or platform re-uploads
   - C2PA spec v2.3: [spec.c2pa.org](https://spec.c2pa.org/specifications/specifications/2.3/specs/C2PA_Specification.html)

2. **Imperceptible Watermarking**
   - Pixel-level or frequency-based changes interwoven with content
   - Survives compression, cropping, editing
   - More robust than metadata but technically harder for text

3. **Fingerprinting**
   - Ex-post identification when active marking fails
   - Logging-based; used as fallback detection method

### Text Content Special Case

For **text content**, the Code allows **digitally signed "Provenance Certificates"** instead of embedded watermarks, acknowledging that text watermarking degrades output quality.

### Two-Tier Disclosure Taxonomy

The Code defines two visible labels:
- **"Fully AI-generated"** — content created autonomously without human-authored authentic content
- **"AI-assisted"** — mixed human-AI involvement affecting meaning, accuracy, or emotional tone

### C2PA (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity)

- Open technical standard, now at v2.3
- ~100 member companies (Adobe, Microsoft, Google, BBC, etc.)
- Adobe is the leading implementer via Content Credentials
- `digitalSourceType` field identifies whether actions were performed by humans or AI/ML systems
- Mandatory metadata: Provider Name, System Version, Creation Timestamp, Unique Identifier

**C2PA is emerging as THE standard** for Article 50(2) compliance, especially for image/video content. For text, Provenance Certificates are the likely path.

### Required Commitments for Providers (from Draft Code)

- Implement marking **"by design"** before market placement
- Ensure content marking across workflows
- Provide default interface-level labeling options
- Offer **free detection tools** with confidence scores
- Maintain **audit-ready compliance documentation**

## 5. Recommendations for Codepliant v3.0

### What Our AI Disclosure Generator Should Produce

Based on this research, an Article 50-compliant AI Disclosure document needs these sections:

1. **AI System Identification** (Art. 50(1))
   - Statement that the product uses AI
   - List of AI systems/models used (detected from code)
   - Purpose of each AI component

2. **Human-AI Interaction Notice** (Art. 50(1))
   - Clear statement when users interact with AI (not a human)
   - Must be shown at first interaction, not buried

3. **Synthetic Content Marking** (Art. 50(2)) — conditional
   - If the product generates images/audio/video/text via AI
   - What marking methods are used (C2PA, watermarking, provenance certificates)
   - Recommendation to implement C2PA Content Credentials

4. **Emotion Recognition / Biometric Disclosure** (Art. 50(3)) — conditional
   - If biometric or emotion recognition services detected
   - Statement that individuals are informed of system operation
   - GDPR cross-reference

5. **Deep Fake / Synthetic Media Disclosure** (Art. 50(4)) — conditional
   - If image/video generation capabilities detected
   - Disclosure that content is artificially generated
   - Applicable exceptions noted

6. **AI Model & Provider Details** (best practice from industry analysis)
   - Which AI providers/subprocessors are used (detected from code)
   - Data handling per provider
   - Training data policies (does the provider train on user data?)

7. **Data Practices for AI Features** (best practice)
   - What data is sent to AI systems
   - Retention periods
   - User opt-out/control options

8. **Risk Classification** (best practice)
   - Self-assessment: minimal/limited/high risk under AI Act
   - Based on detected use patterns

9. **Responsible AI Principles** (best practice from Grammarly/GitHub pattern)
   - Brief statement of principles (transparency, fairness, accountability, etc.)

10. **Contact & Compliance Information**
    - AI compliance officer / DPO
    - How to raise concerns
    - Last updated date

### New Service Signatures Needed

To detect Art. 50-relevant services, we should add signatures for:
- Image generation: `@stability-ai/sdk`, `openai` (DALL-E), `midjourney`, `replicate`
- Video generation: `@runwayml/sdk`, `synthesia`
- Voice/audio synthesis: `elevenlabs`, `@google-cloud/text-to-speech`, `amazon-polly`
- Emotion recognition: `face-api.js`, `@aws-sdk/client-rekognition`, `azure-ai-vision`
- Biometric: fingerprint/face auth libraries
- Text generation: already covered (openai, anthropic, etc.)
