## 1. CCPA/CPRA Requirements (2026)

### 1.1 Required Privacy Policy Sections

A CCPA/CPRA-compliant privacy policy must include these mandatory disclosures:

1. **Categories of personal information collected** in the past 12 months, mapped to the CCPA's statutory categories
2. **Categories of sensitive personal information collected** (expanded list, see 1.3)
3. **Purposes for collection and use** per category
4. **Whether each category is sold or shared** with third parties
5. **Categories of third parties** to whom PI is disclosed
6. **Retention periods or criteria** for each data category
7. **Consumer rights section** explaining:
   - Right to know / access
   - Right to delete
   - Right to correct
   - Right to opt out of sale/sharing
   - Right to limit use of sensitive PI
   - Right to non-discrimination
8. **"Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information"** link (if applicable)
9. **"Limit the Use of My Sensitive Personal Information"** link (if applicable)
10. **How opt-out preference signals (GPC) are processed** — must explain
11. **Date the policy was last updated**
12. **Accessibility** — available in languages matching the business's consumer-facing languages; accessible to users with disabilities
13. **Notice at collection** linkage — direct links to relevant sections

### 1.2 "Do Not Sell or Share" Requirement

- Businesses must provide a visible, accessible **"Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information"** link on their website
- As of 2026, businesses must display **visible confirmation signals** such as "Opt-Out Request Honored" toggles, badges, or messages
- "Sharing" includes disclosing PI to advertising partners, analytics providers, and social networks for cross-context behavioral advertising — even without monetary exchange
- Consumers can exercise opt-out via: the DNSS link, GPC signals, or combined opt-out mechanisms
- **Authorized agents** may submit opt-out requests on behalf of consumers (with power of attorney)
- Businesses must process opt-out requests within **15 business days**

### 1.3 Sensitive Personal Information Categories

The CCPA/CPRA defines these sensitive PI categories:

1. Social Security number, driver's license, state ID, passport number
2. Account log-in credentials (username + password/security question)
3. Financial account number with access code (debit/credit card + PIN)
4. Precise geolocation data
5. Racial or ethnic origin
6. Religious or philosophical beliefs
7. Union membership
8. Contents of mail, email, text messages (unless business is the intended recipient)
9. Genetic data
10. Biometric data for identification purposes
11. Health information
12. Sex life or sexual orientation data
13. **Neural data** (added 2024) — information measuring central or peripheral nervous system activity (EEG readings, brain-computer interface data)
14. **Data relating to consumers under 16** (added as sensitive under CPRA enforcement)

Consumers have the right to **limit** how businesses use and disclose sensitive PI.

### 1.4 Universal Opt-Out Mechanism (UOOM)

- **Global Privacy Control (GPC)** is the primary recognized UOOM
- Businesses must detect, honor, and confirm GPC signals with **visible indicators** — not silent processing
- **AB 566 "Opt Me Out" Act** (signed October 2025): requires all web browsers to provide built-in opt-out preference signals, effective **January 1, 2027**
- The UOOM applies to both sale AND sharing of personal information
- When a logged-in user has conflicting account settings vs GPC signal, businesses may notify the user of the conflict and give them an opportunity to consent

### 1.5 CCPA's 11 Statutory Categories of Personal Information

1. Identifiers (name, address, email, IP, SSN, etc.)
2. Customer records (financial info, insurance, employment history)
3. Protected classifications (age, race, gender, disability, etc.)
4. Commercial information (purchase history, products/services considered)
5. Biometric information
6. Internet/network activity (browsing history, search history, interaction data)
7. Geolocation data
8. Sensory data (audio, visual, thermal, olfactory)
9. Professional/employment information
10. Education information (non-public FERPA records)
11. Inferences drawn from PI to create a consumer profile

## 2. UK GDPR vs EU GDPR Differences

### 2.1 Supervisory Authority

| | EU GDPR | UK GDPR |
|---|---|---|
| **Authority** | Multiple national DPAs across member states | ICO (sole authority) |
| **One-Stop-Shop** | Available for cross-border processing | Does NOT apply — direct relationship with ICO |
| **Complaints** | To any EU DPA | To ICO only |

### 2.2 Key Regulatory Differences

| Area | EU GDPR | UK GDPR (post-DUAA) |
|---|---|---|
| **Age of consent** | 16 (member states can lower to 13) | **13** |
| **Cookie consent** | Strict prior consent required for all non-essential cookies | **New exemptions**: analytics cookies (with opt-out), security cookies, functional cookies exempt from consent |
| **Legitimate interest** | Always requires balancing test (Art. 6(1)(f)) | **New Art. 6(1)(ea)**: "recognised legitimate interests" with NO balancing test for crime prevention, safeguarding, emergencies, national security |
| **Automated decisions** | Art. 22: explicit consent required for automated decisions using special category data | **Relaxed**: safeguards (notice, contestation, human intervention) sufficient — no explicit consent needed |
| **DSARs** | Extensions only for "exceptional" circumstances | **"Stop the clock"** provision: controllers can pause timelines to gather information from requester |
| **Right to complain** | Existing right to lodge complaint with DPA | **New formal right**: 30-day acknowledgment, "without undue delay" full response (from June 19, 2026) |
| **Penalties (e-privacy)** | Varies by member state | **Raised to 4% global turnover** (previously capped at £500,000) — now matches UK GDPR caps |
| **Research purposes** | Broad consent permitted | Must **re-consent** if original basis was consent; new exemption from notices for disproportionate effort |
| **Direct marketing** | Legitimate interest sometimes argued | **Explicitly clarified** as potential legitimate interest |

### 2.3 UK International Data Transfers (IDTA vs SCCs)

**Three mechanisms for UK transfers:**

1. **UK IDTA (International Data Transfer Agreement)**: Standalone UK-specific agreement
   - Single template for ALL transfer types (C2C, C2P, P2P, P2C)
   - No separate modules (unlike EU SCCs)
   - UK-only — cannot be used for EU transfers

2. **UK Addendum to EU SCCs**: Modifies EU SCCs for UK compliance
   - Preferred by organizations already using EU SCCs
   - Simpler for dual UK+EU compliance

3. **Adequacy decisions**: UK makes its own, independent of EU
   - Current EU adequacy of UK lasts until **December 27, 2031**

**DUAA changes to transfers:**
- "Data protection test" replaces "adequacy decision" terminology
- New standard: whether destination protections are **"not materially lower"** than UK standards — more flexible than EU's "essentially equivalent" test
- ICO plans to update IDTA and Addendum in 2026

**EU-US Data Privacy Framework (DPF):**
- UK Extension to EU-US DPF available for US transfers
- Most major tech companies certified

### 2.4 UK-Specific Post-Brexit Requirements

- **UK Representative (Art. 27)**: Non-UK organizations must appoint a UK representative, separate from any EU representative
- **ICO registration**: Data controllers must register with ICO (annual fee)
- **Children's Code**: ICO's Age Appropriate Design Code formalized — services "likely to be used by children" must account for children's needs; compliance with the Code satisfies DUAA requirements
- **Dual compliance mandatory**: UK businesses with EEA presence/customers must comply with BOTH UK and EU frameworks

### 2.5 Practical Impact for Privacy Policy Generation

For a multi-jurisdiction policy covering both EU and UK:
- **Separate DPA references**: "ICO" for UK, relevant EU DPA for EU
- **Separate representative contacts**: UK Art. 27 rep AND EU Art. 27 rep
- **Transfer mechanisms**: List both SCCs (EU) and IDTA/UK Addendum
- **Cookie section**: Note UK's relaxed consent for analytics (with opt-out)
- **Age of consent**: 13 (UK) vs 16 (EU default)
- **Legal basis differences**: UK recognised legitimate interests don't require balancing test
- **Right to complain**: UK's new formal 30-day acknowledgment requirement

## 3. Global Privacy Control (GPC)

### 3.1 Technical Implementation

**JavaScript API Detection:**
```javascript
if (navigator.globalPrivacyControl) {
  // User has enabled GPC — honor opt-out
}
```

**HTTP Header:**
- `Sec-GPC: 1` header sent with every request when user has GPC enabled
- Stateless protocol: every request contains the signal, no server-side state needed

**.well-known Compliance File:**
```json
// /.well-known/gpc.json
{
  "gpc": true,
  "lastUpdate": "2026-03-15"
}
```
Existence of this file signals the site honors GPC.

**IAB USP API Integration:**
```javascript
var uspFramework = {
  version: 1,
  notice: 'Y',
  optOut: CCPAConsent,  // Set from GPC signal
  lspa: 'Y'
}
```

### 3.2 States Requiring GPC/UOOM Recognition (as of Jan 1, 2026)

12 states mandate recognition of universal opt-out mechanisms:

1. California
2. Colorado
3. Connecticut
4. Delaware
5. Maryland
6. Minnesota
7. Montana
8. Nebraska
9. New Hampshire
10. New Jersey
11. Oregon
12. Texas

California, Colorado, and Connecticut have **explicitly confirmed** GPC qualifies as a UOOM.

### 3.3 How Apps Should Detect and Respond to GPC

**Detection (place as early in page load as possible):**
1. Check `navigator.globalPrivacyControl` (JavaScript)
2. Check `Sec-GPC` HTTP header (server-side)

**Response requirements:**
1. Honor opt-out **immediately** for unidentified users
2. Display visible confirmation: "Opt-Out Request Honored" or similar
3. Apply opt-outs across **all devices and accounts** for logged-in users
4. Maintain **audit trails** documenting compliance
5. If logged-in user has conflicting account settings, **notify user of conflict** and give opportunity to consent

**Privacy policy must:**
- State that GPC signals are honored
- Explain how the signal is processed
- Example language: "If your browser supports it, you can turn on the Global Privacy Control to opt out of the sale or sharing of your personal information."

### 3.4 Enforcement Actions (Notable)

| Company | Date | Fine | Violation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Healthline Media | July 2025 | $1.55M | Failed to honor GPC opt-out requests |
| Tractor Supply | Aug 2025 | $1.35M | Did not process GPC signals until July 2024 |
| Honda | March 2025 | $632.5K | Failed to apply GPC signals to user accounts |
| Sling TV | Oct 2025 | $530K | Made opt-out confusing and inaccessible |

### 3.5 Browser Support

- **Currently supported:** Firefox, Brave, DuckDuckGo, and browser extensions
- **Coming Jan 1, 2027:** AB 566 requires Chrome, Safari, and Edge to offer built-in GPC

## 4. Real-World Multi-Jurisdiction Privacy Policies

### 4.1 Stripe (US + EU + UK + 16+ jurisdictions)

**Architecture: Unified policy with jurisdiction-specific appendix**

**Main section structure:**
1. Defined Terms
2. Collection & Use by User Type (End Users, End Customers, Representatives, Visitors)
3. Additional Collection & Use Methods
4. Legal Bases for Processing
5. Rights & Choices
6. Security & Retention
7. Updates & Notifications
8. **Jurisdiction-Specific Provisions** (the key multi-jurisdiction section)
9. Contact Information
10. US Consumer Privacy Notice (Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act)

**How they handle multi-jurisdiction:**
- **Section 8** has 16+ subsections: EEA, UK, US, Australia, Brazil, Canada, Japan, India, Thailand, Singapore, and more
- Each subsection tailors legal bases, rights mechanisms, and transfer safeguards to local requirements
- Separate DPO contact (dpo@stripe.com) for GDPR inquiries
- EEA complaints to Irish DPC; UK complaints to ICO
- **CCPA handling:** Dedicated subsection with right to know, opt-out of sale/sharing, limit sensitive data use, verification process, authorized agent provisions
- **Transfer mechanisms:** EU SCCs + UK IDTA Addendum + DPF + CBPR certifications
- Separate Privacy Center for region-specific privacy controls

### 4.2 Shopify (US + EU + UK + global)

**Architecture: Tiered approach — base policy + supplemental notices**

**Main section structure:**
1. Introduction (four user categories)
2. Our Values (three guiding principles)
3. Why We Process Your Information
4. Your Rights Over Your Information
5. Where We Send Your Information
6. How Long We Retain Your Information
7. Our Use of Machine Learning
8. How We Protect Your Information
9. Cookies and Tracking Technologies
10. How to Reach Us
11. **United States Regional Privacy Notice** (appendix)

**How they handle multi-jurisdiction:**
- **Base policy** covers GDPR/UK GDPR with embedded legal bases, rights, transfer mechanisms
- **US Regional Notice** is a separate appendix addressing CCPA + state laws (CA, CO, CT, UT, VA)
- Three contact addresses by geography: Canada/US, EEA/UK/Middle East/Africa/South America, Asia/Australia/NZ
- DPO contact for EEA/UK/Switzerland
- Transfer chain: EEA/UK → Ireland subsidiary → Canada (adequacy) → US (SCCs)
- Do Not Track: explicitly states signals NOT honored (interesting contrast)

### 4.3 Atlassian (US + EU + UK + global)

**Architecture: Unified policy with regional disclosures section**

**Main section structure:**
1. Information We Collect
2. How We Use Information
3. How We Disclose Information
4. Storage and Security
5. Data Retention
6. Access and Control Rights
7. Children's Policy
8. **Regional Disclosures** (jurisdiction-specific)
9. Policy Changes
10. Contact Information

**How they handle multi-jurisdiction:**
- **Regional Disclosures** section contains two subsections:
  - **EEA/UK subsection:** Legal bases table (Art. 6 GDPR), rights, separate EU and UK representatives, transfer mechanisms, DPF certification
  - **US State subsection:** 12-month disclosure table mapping categories to purposes/recipients, selling/sharing distinctions, GPC support, authorized agent provisions
- Separate EU representative and UK representative contact details
- TRUSTe dispute resolution for DPF complaints
- **GPC: explicitly honored** (unlike Shopify)
- Sensitive PI carve-out: "We do not use or disclose sensitive personal information for purposes other than permitted under applicable law"

### 4.4 Multi-Jurisdiction Design Patterns (Summary)

All three companies use the same fundamental pattern:

| Pattern | Description | Used By |
|---|---|---|
| **Unified base + jurisdiction appendix** | One global policy with regional sections at the end | Stripe, Atlassian |
| **Tiered base + supplemental** | Base policy + separate regional notice | Shopify |
| **Per-topic embedding** | Jurisdiction-specific notes inline within each section | All three (for GDPR legal bases) |

**Common structural elements across all three:**
1. Global rights section (covers the union of all jurisdictions)
2. DPO/contact per region (EEA DPO, UK representative, US privacy team)
3. Transfer mechanisms section (SCCs + DPF + IDTA/Addendum)
4. CCPA-specific appendix or subsection with:
   - 12-month data category disclosure table
   - Sale/sharing disclosure
   - Consumer rights specific to CCPA
   - GPC/opt-out signal handling
5. Separate representatives for EU and UK
6. Children's section with age thresholds per jurisdiction

**Best practice for Codepliant v4.0:** Follow the **unified base + jurisdiction appendix** pattern. Generate one document with a core section (applicable everywhere) and conditional jurisdiction-specific sections at the bottom (EU/EEA, UK, US/CCPA, etc.) based on detected configuration.

## 5. Competitor Analysis: Multi-Jurisdiction Privacy Policy Generators

### 5.1 Existing Tools

| Tool | Approach | Jurisdictions | Price | Differentiator |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Termly** | Questionnaire → hosted policy | GDPR, CCPA, 28+ laws | Free tier + paid | Auto-updates when laws change; hosted URL |
| **Termageddon** | Attorney-reviewed questionnaire | GDPR, CPRA, UK DPA, all US states | Paid ($10+/mo) | IAPP-listed vendor; attorney-founded; auto-updates |
| **iubenda** | 2000+ lawyer-drafted clauses | 15+ languages, multi-region | Freemium | Clause library; cookie scanning; multi-language |
| **PrivacyStatementGenerator.com** | Region-selection questionnaire | EU, UK, CA, Canada, etc. | Free | Select all applicable regions → combined policy |
| **Usercentrics** | Platform + CMP | Multi-region | Enterprise | Combined consent + policy management |
| **GetTerms.io** | Questionnaire | GDPR, CCPA | Free tier | Simple and fast |

### 5.2 Gap Analysis — Where Codepliant Differentiates

**No existing tool generates privacy policies from code scanning.** Every competitor uses questionnaires or manual input. Key differentiators for Codepliant v4.0:

1. **Code-scanning accuracy**: Detect actual data practices from dependencies/imports, not self-reported answers
2. **Multi-jurisdiction from config**: User specifies `jurisdictions: ["EU", "UK", "CCPA"]` and we generate the appropriate sections automatically
3. **Auto-detection of data flows**: We already detect third-party services (Stripe, analytics, AI providers) and can map them to jurisdiction-specific disclosures
4. **GPC disclosure**: We can detect if the app handles GPC signals and generate appropriate disclosure language
5. **Transfer mechanism awareness**: Based on detected US-based providers, auto-include SCCs/IDTA/DPF sections
6. **Re-scanning**: Unlike static generators, re-run to update when code changes — no questionnaire to redo

### 5.3 Competitor Weaknesses We Can Exploit

- Questionnaire fatigue: users often skip or answer inaccurately
- Static output: most don't re-scan or auto-update based on actual code
- One-size-fits-all templates: don't adapt to what the app actually does
- No AI disclosure integration: none generate EU AI Act + privacy policy together
- No DPA generation: most only do privacy policy, not the full compliance document set

## 6. Recommendations for v4.0 Implementation

### 6.1 Config Changes

```typescript
interface CodepliantConfig {
  // Existing fields...

  // v4.0: Multi-jurisdiction
  jurisdictions?: ('EU' | 'UK' | 'CCPA' | 'LGPD' | 'PIPEDA')[];

  // CCPA-specific
  sellsPersonalInformation?: boolean;
  sharesCrossBehavioralAds?: boolean;
  collectsSensitivePI?: boolean;

  // UK-specific
  ukRepresentative?: string;
  icoRegistrationNumber?: string;

  // EU-specific (already have some)
  euRepresentative?: string;
  dpoName?: string;
  dpoEmail?: string;

  // GPC
  honorsGPC?: boolean;
}
```

### 6.2 Privacy Policy Generator Architecture

Follow the **unified base + jurisdiction appendix** pattern:

**Core sections (always generated):**
1. Introduction & Data Controller Identity
2. Information We Collect (from scanning)
3. How We Use Your Information
4. How We Share Your Information
5. Data Retention
6. Your Rights (superset of all jurisdictions)
7. Security
8. Cookies and Tracking
9. Children's Privacy
10. Changes to This Policy
11. Contact Information

**Conditional jurisdiction sections (appended based on `jurisdictions` config):**

**If "EU" in jurisdictions:**
- EU/EEA Residents section
- Legal basis per purpose table (Art. 6 GDPR)
- EU representative contact
- DPO contact
- SCCs + DPF transfer mechanisms
- Right to complain to supervisory authority
- Automated decision-making disclosure (if AI detected)

**If "UK" in jurisdictions:**
- UK Residents section
- UK representative contact (separate from EU)
- ICO as supervisory authority
- IDTA/UK Addendum transfer mechanisms
- UK cookie consent differences (analytics exempt with opt-out)
- Age of consent: 13
- Right to complain (30-day acknowledgment per DUAA)

**If "CCPA" in jurisdictions:**
- California / US State Privacy Notice
- 12-month data category disclosure table
- Categories of PI sold/shared
- Sensitive PI categories collected
- Consumer rights (know, delete, correct, opt-out, limit sensitive PI, non-discrimination)
- "Do Not Sell or Share" link reference
- "Limit Use of Sensitive PI" link reference
- GPC signal handling disclosure
- Authorized agent provisions
- Verification process

### 6.3 GPC Detection and Disclosure

If the scanned codebase contains references to `navigator.globalPrivacyControl`, `Sec-GPC`, or `gpc.json`, auto-generate GPC compliance language. If `honorsGPC` config is true, generate the disclosure regardless.

### 6.4 Transfer Mechanism Logic

Based on detected providers:
- If any US-based provider detected AND EU/UK jurisdiction enabled:
  - Include SCCs section (EU)
  - Include IDTA/UK Addendum section (UK)
  - Include DPF reference
- Map providers to countries: Stripe (US), Anthropic (US), etc.
- Generate transfer safeguard table
